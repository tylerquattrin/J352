College Park considers accessory dwelling unit policy under new state law

By Tyler Quattrin | 7 May 2026

The exterior of College Park City Hall. (Tyler Quattrin)

As College Park considers how to respond to a new state law regarding accessory dwelling units, city leaders are discussing whether the policy will help improve the affordable housing crisis.

The Maryland Accessory Dwelling Units Act of 2025 requires local governments to allow at least one accessory dwelling unit on lots with single-family homes.

Accessory dwelling units are small, independent residential units located on the same property as a primary home. They can include a basement apartment or detached backyard cottages, and must have their own kitchen, bathroom, entrance and sleeping area.

In Prince George’s County, a task force is currently developing regulations to determine how accessory dwelling units must be implemented.

At a recent meeting, the College Park City Council voted to send a letter to the county’s ADU Task Force outlining its priorities. City officials said they want to support additional housing options while addressing concerns from residents about neighborhood impacts.

Accessory dwelling units could expand housing supply in the city, where demand continues to remain high, according to city officials.

College Park mayor Fazlul Kabir said accessory dwelling units can provide diversified housing options, help homeowners generate additional income and potentially provide more affordable rentals in the city.

Still, he said, “we also want to ensure that any changes align with neighborhood character, infrastructure capacity and our broader housing goals.”

Kabir emphasized that those decisions are made at the county level, and the city’s role is to reflect the needs of its residents.

Other major cities that have adopted ADU-friendly policies, including Portland and Los Angeles, have added thousands of units in recent years.

The city of Long Beach, California, reported that between 2016 and 2024, the city went from zero to more than 800 accessory dwelling units by advertising to homeowners and permit streamlining. The city wrote in a Los Angeles Times article that its investments paid off, with the increased housing supply resulting in rent burden relief.

Listing data shows that as of September 2025, rents are down across Long Beach, compared with steeper hikes experienced by renters elsewhere in the area.

Still, many College Park residents have raised concerns about accessory dwelling units coming to the city.

District 1 council member Jacob Hernandez said an approach to accessory dwelling units must be strategic, raising concerns about increased demand.

“If this does end up rolling out, we need to make sure we have the proper infrastructure in place in order to capture this additional revenue source that probably exists, so that we can fund the city services that are going to be used here,” he said. “Everything from trash to parking.”

While Hernandez said he does believe accessory dwelling units can improve affordability, he also raised concerns for tenant safety.

“Obviously, there are many ADUs built throughout the city and the county that are not built up to code,” he said. “Just because it’s affordable doesn’t mean it’s safe.”

In its letter to the task force, the city recommends that the task force adopt specific standards for College Park as a college town.

Recommendations include a minimum off-street parking standard of two parking spaces per unit, no more than one accessory dwelling unit per lot, a frequent inspection process and a size limit of 1,000 square feet or 10% of the lot area.

College Park’s affordable housing task force released a report last month, which listed accessory dwelling units as one way to address affordable housing issues. The report indicated that developing an ADU policy that encourages building and requires owner occupancy of one of the structures on the lot is of high importance for the city.

However, the report also noted that while ADU reform is important, its effects are currently unknown because of the recency of legislation.
